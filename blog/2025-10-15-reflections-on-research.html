Reflections on Research: Bridging Theory and Practice

October 15, 2025 by Catarina Gamboa

What does it mean to do meaningful research in software engineering? This question has been on my mind lately, especially as I reflect on my journey through the dual Ph.D. program and the work I've been doing on liquid types and software verification.

The Theory-Practice Gap

One of the biggest challenges in programming languages and software verification research is the gap between theoretical advances and practical adoption. We can develop powerful type systems and verification techniques in academia, but if developers can't or won't use them, what impact do they really have?

This realization has shaped my approach to research. It's not enough to prove that a technique is theoretically sound or that it can verify certain properties. We need to ask: Is it usable? Is it intuitive? Does it fit into developers' workflows?

Usability in Formal Methods

My work on LiquidJava has taught me that usability isn't a secondary concern—it's fundamental. Through user studies and iterative design, I've learned that even small interface decisions can make or break a tool's adoption. Error messages, IDE integration, the learning curve—all of these factors matter just as much as the underlying theory.

"The best verification technique is the one that developers will actually use."

The Interdisciplinary Nature of Modern Research

What excites me most about my research area is its inherently interdisciplinary nature. To build better verification tools, I need to understand:

  • Type theory and formal methods
  • Human-computer interaction principles
  • Software engineering practices
  • Cognitive psychology (how developers think and learn)

This intersection is challenging, but it's also where the most impactful work happens. By bringing together insights from multiple fields, we can create tools that are both powerful and practical.

Looking Forward

As I continue my Ph.D. journey, I'm committed to research that bridges the theory-practice gap. I want to build tools that not only advance the state of the art but also make a real difference in how software is developed and verified.

The road ahead is long, but I'm energized by the challenges and the potential for real-world impact. After all, the ultimate goal of our research should be to make software more reliable and developers more productive—not just to publish papers.

— Catarina
